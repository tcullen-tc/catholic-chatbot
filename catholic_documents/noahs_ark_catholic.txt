NOAH'S ARK IN CATHOLIC TEACHING

The story of Noah and the Ark (Genesis 6-9) is understood in Catholic teaching as a type (foreshadowing) of baptism and salvation through Christ.

CCC 56-58: After the unity of the human race was shattered by sin, God sought to save humanity through the family of Noah. The covenant with Noah was a preparation for the universal salvation to come.

CCC 701: The dove with the olive branch symbolizes the peace of the soul reconciled with God, prefiguring the Holy Spirit who descends upon Jesus at his baptism.

CCC 845: The Church is the "ark" of salvation, prefigured by Noah's Ark. Just as the Ark saved Noah and his family from the flood, the Church saves those who enter through baptism.

CATECHISM TEACHING:
The Flood is not merely a historical event but a theological symbol. St. Peter writes: "God waited patiently in the days of Noah during the building of the ark, in which a few, that is, eight persons, were saved through water. Baptism, which corresponds to this, now saves you" (1 Peter 3:20-21).

THE MEANING:
The Ark represents the Church, which preserves humanity from sin and death. The flood waters represent both judgment and new life in baptism. The dove returning with the olive branch symbolizes the Holy Spirit bringing peace and reconciliation.

Sources: Catechism of the Catholic Church 56-58, 701, 845; 1 Peter 3:20-21
